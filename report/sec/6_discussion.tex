\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Why are absolute numbers so close to random?} Object \emph{state} -- deformation, contact, orientation change -- is underrepresented in web-scale VLM training data relative to object \emph{identity} and \emph{position}. Models can usually describe which objects are present and where, but not reliably what has happened to them or which phase of an action is depicted. Compounding the problem, the models rarely use ``Cannot be determined'' as an answer even when it is correct, defaulting instead to a
confident Yes or No (\cref{sec:results-lora}: BASE Qwen3-4B scores 0--15\% on the CnBD
variant). High values of small models, such as 70\%-plus \texttt{task\_success} accuracy of the Qwen3 can not be safely attributed to exceptional performance, as the model could by accident confideently mimic the the majority label distribution. 

\textbf{Scale helps, but is not enough.} Within-family scaling consistently improves
accuracy (Gemma3 E2B~$\to$~E4B~$\to$~31B), but even 31B/32B-class models plateau in the
high-40s\% on phase-level reasoning. Cross-family comparison shows, this hypothesis holds, InternVL3-14B is competitive with
Qwen3-8B despite the parameter gap, suggesting architecture and training data matter at
least as much as raw parameter count. More parameters alone are unlikely to close the
gap; robot-domain training data is needed.

\textbf{LoRA gains are misrepresentative.} The 11pp improvement suggest ``fine-tuning works for robot state reasoning,'' but the
decomposition in \cref{sec:results-lora} shows that roughly 80\% of the gain is two
coarse heuristics. The model learns to detect if a random scene compared to the task is shown, implying that the vision-backbone signal can be tuned effectively for such detection. Yet it is domain-mismatch detection, not phase or temporal reasoning. The other gain is due %TODO control if gain in action phase detection is stronger in action phase context giving
label-position bias on the \texttt{phase\_success} is net positive under this dataset's label distribution, but a regression for the most safety-critical use case: detecting when a robot is currently in the middle of an action. The results show that at this scale no improvement of reasoning can be seen, but mostly better dissecting of the vision-backbone signal. 

\textbf{Multi-view consistency tracks confidence, not correctness.} Models are most
self-consistent when uniformly right (72.3\%) or uniformly wrong (66.4\%) across views,
and least consistent when only some views are correct (34.4\%). We read this as
view-consistency reflecting how unambiguous a scene is to the model, not whether its
answer is correct. This connects to the scene-difficulty finding (\cref{sec:results}):
scenes that drive accuracy down also destabilize cross-view agreement, suggesting both
effects share the same root cause -- visual ambiguity, not task semantics. For multi-view
fusion, this implies that simple voting across views may not help on exactly the hardest
scenes, where inconsistency is highest.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Accuracy Analysis.} Object state --- such as contact or orientation change --- and temporal understanding are underrepresented in web-scale VLM training data relative to object identification and position estimation. Models can usually describe which objects are present and where, but not reliably what has happened to them or which phase of an action is depicted. The vision backbone encoding is not influenced by the prompt; the encoded signal must therefore suffice for answering on its own.
High \texttt{task\_success} accuracy in the Qwen3 family (70\%+) cannot be safely attributed to genuine perception, as these models may exhibit high-confidence convergence on the majority label.

\textbf{Scale difference.} Scaling consistently improves accuracy across all families, but even 31B/32B-class models plateau in the high-40s\% on phase-level reasoning. Cross-family comparisons support this: Qwen3-8B is competitive with InternVL3-14B despite the parameter gap, suggesting architecture and training data matter at least as much as raw parameter count. From these results it is difficult to determine whether the vision backbone, training data, or vision-language alignment is the primary bottleneck. Nonetheless, more parameters alone are unlikely to close the gap; dedicated robot-domain training data appears necessary.

\textbf{LoRA fine-tuning.} Although LoRA improves overall accuracy by 11pp, the decomposition in \cref{sec:results-lora} shows that roughly 80\% of the gain is attributed to two coarse heuristics rather than stronger perception capabilities. The model learns to detect if a random scene compared to the task is shown, implying that the vision-backbone signal can be leveraged effectively for such detection. This generalizes across train, val, and test splits on \texttt{action\_phase\_id} and \texttt{task\_success}, ruling out simple memorization --- but it is domain-mismatch detection, not phase or temporal reasoning. The other gain is in \texttt{phase\_success} at MIDDLE phase positions; because these questions include the ordered list of action phases as context, we believe the model is improving at reasoning from that list rather than from the image itself.

This \texttt{phase\_success} gain is net positive under the dataset's label distribution, but a regression for the most safety-critical use case: detecting when a robot is currently mid-action. At this scale, the gains reflect better exploitation of the vision-backbone signal rather than improved reasoning.

\textbf{CoT prompting.}
CoT traces show the models can describe and identify individual views, but struggle with basics such as correct view alignment and depth estimation. Single-letter prompts force a direct guess that happens to land closer to the skewed label distribution (72\% ``No'' on \texttt{task\_success}), while CoT shifts the model toward expressing uncertainty regardless of visual clarity. Judging by the reasoning chains, Gemma3-E2B and E4B appear to lack a clear internal representation of the depicted scenes.

\textbf{Multi-view consistency tracks confidence, not correctness.} Models are most self-consistent when uniformly right (72.3\%) or uniformly wrong (66.4\%) across views, and least consistent when only some views are correct (34.4\%). We read this as
view-consistency reflecting how unambiguous a scene is to the model, not whether its
answer is correct. This connects to the scene-difficulty finding (\cref{sec:results}):
scenes that drive accuracy down also destabilize cross-view agreement, suggesting both
effects share the same root causes. For multi-view fusion, this implies that simple voting across views may not help on exactly the hardest scenes, where inconsistency is highest.

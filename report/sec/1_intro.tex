Recent advances in Vision-Language Models~(VLMs) have pushed strong object-recognition and coarse spatial reasoning into robotics, but state-change, contact, and temporal reasoning remain comparatively weak~\cite{lin2025vlmsurvey}. This gap matters directly for robot supervision, which depends on knowing whether a grasp succeeded, where a task stands mid-execution, and whether the current action phase is on track.

We probe two concrete facets of this problem. The first is \emph{failure-mode detection}: given images from multiple viewpoints, can a VLM identify the current action phase, judge whether that phase succeeded, and estimate overall task progress? The second is \emph{multi-view consistency}: when the same scene moment is captured from two synchronized cameras, do the model's answers agree? A model that reasons about the same scene differently depending on which camera it sees is unreliable as a supervisor regardless of its average accuracy.

To study both, we build a new benchmark based on Robo2VLM-1~\cite{chen2025robo2vlm} (DROID~\cite{khazatsky2024droid} and OXE trajectories), comprising 6224 failure-mode and 6902 multi-view consistency questions across 47 hand-labeled scenes and five self-designed question types (action phase, phase success, progress, task success, and multi-view consistency). We evaluate seven VLMs from three families (Gemma3, Qwen3, InternVL3) zero-shot, ablate chain-of-thought~(CoT) prompting, break results down by scene difficulty, and run a LoRA fine-tuning case study on Qwen3-4B.

The results paint a sobering picture: zero-shot accuracy of small models hovers near chance, CoT prompting is inconsistent and often harmful, and LoRA fine-tuning gains decompose mostly into learned answer-distribution shortcuts rather than improved scene understanding. We trace where performance collapses as task and scene complexity increase.

\begin{abstract}
Vision-Language-Models (VLMs) are increasingly proposed action-prediction models and supervisors for
robotic manipulation. Both demand judgment upon task progress and detecting failures from camera input within the context of the task. 
We benchmark these capabilities with a curated dataset: (1)
failure-mode and state reasoning from a single image and a stated goal, and (2)
consistency of answers extracted from different camera viewpoints of the same moment.
Using a new benchmark built on Robo2VLM-1 (DROID + OXE; 47 scenes, 6224 questions, 4
question types), we evaluate seven VLMs across three families (Gemma3, Qwen3, InternVL3)
zero-shot, with a chain-of-thought (CoT) ablation and a LoRA fine-tuning case study. We
find that (i) zero-shot accuracy is only marginally above random (high-40s\% vs.\ 33\%
uniform random) and plateaus even at 30B+ scale; (ii) CoT prompting introduces secondary complications; and (iii) LoRA
fine-tuning yields a sizeable +10.9pp headline gain on Qwen3-4B, but roughly 80\% of it
decomposes into two coarse heuristics -- a generalizable out-of-distribution scene
detector and a label-position bias -- rather than improved phase or temporal reasoning. Our results suggest current small- and mid-sized VLMs are not yet reliable robot-state
supervisors out of the box, and that fine-tuning gains require careful control.
\end{abstract}

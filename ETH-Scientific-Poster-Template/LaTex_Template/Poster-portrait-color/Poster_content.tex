% ====================== INTRODUCTION ===================
%% 
\def\introtext
{\Large \black
Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have emerged as promising candidates for robot supervision --- capable of interpreting scenes and providing semantic feedback without task-specific training. This work benchmarks two concrete capabilities: \textbf{multi-view consistency} (can a VLM extract coherent spatial information across camera angles?) and \textbf{failure mode detection} (can it identify when a robotic task has gone wrong?). We evaluate two model families across both tasks, providing an early empirical signal on where current VLMs succeed and where they fall short as supervisory tools for robotics.
}%% end text

\vspace{6pt}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Marginal gains over random.} Overall accuracy (38.6\,\%) barely exceeds uniform random (33.3\,\%) and falls below the yes/no baseline (39.6\,\%).
  \item \textbf{Task success inflated by label bias.} Qwen models reach 72--73\,\% but match the majority-class baseline --- ``No'' dominates ground truth.
  \item \textbf{Scenes differ substantially in difficulty.} Across the 47 evaluated scenes, Gemma4-31B ranges from 34.7\,\% (rope alignment) to 65.3\,\% (pick-and-place with distinct objects) --- a 31\,pp spread --- suggesting scene complexity and visual ambiguity are strong drivers of model error.
  \item \textbf{Simple manipulation on clear surfaces works best.} Scenes featuring visually distinct objects on uncluttered, high-contrast surfaces yield the highest accuracy. Models appear to benefit from clear foreground--background separation and unambiguous object identity.
\end{itemize}

% ====================== CONCLUSION ===================
%% 
\def\conclusiontext
{\Large \black
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CoT reveals reasoning limits.} Chain-of-thought drops task success to 20.9\,\% (below random), yet shows models \emph{can} articulate scene descriptions and hypothesise actions --- they just frequently draw false conclusions from ambiguous images.
  \item \textbf{Without fine-tuning, scale wins.} Gemma4-31B leads overall (47.5\,\%); smaller variants trail by 10--17\,pp. Until domain adaptation is applied, model capacity remains the dominant factor.
  \item \textbf{Not designed for robotic scene understanding.} Off-the-shelf VLMs excel at object detection and relative positions in pick-and-place scenarios, but struggle with tasks like \textit{scrunch up}, \textit{align}, or \textit{close lid}. We attribute this to insufficient training signal for fine-grained \emph{object state} --- deformation, orientation, and contact --- which rarely appears explicitly in web-scale data.
  \item \textbf{Limitations \& future work.} Manual annotations limit scale; automated pipelines and fine-tuning on robotic manipulation data are the natural next step.
\end{itemize}
